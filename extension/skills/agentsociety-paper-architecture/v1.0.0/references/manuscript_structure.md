# Manuscript Structure Reference

This reference distills the section-specific writing rules for
Nature/Science-level manuscripts. It governs how the architecture
producer structures the section outline and drafts manuscript blocks.

## Title

- Name the **problem**, **finding**, or **mechanism**.
- Do not reduce the title to a dataset description.
- Do not include methodology unless the method itself is the contribution.

## Abstract

Use this pressure curve:

1. **Why this matters** — 1-2 sentences establishing stakes
2. **What is not yet understood** — the precise gap
3. **What we do** — the approach, compressed
4. **What we find** — the central result, stated precisely
5. **What follows from it** — the implication, proportional to evidence

### Abstract Rules

- Each sentence must have one dominant function from the pressure curve.
- Do not bury the finding in the middle.
- Do not end with vague significance ("has important implications").
- Target: 150-200 words for Nature format.

## Introduction (Main)

### Structure

1. **Build pressure** — establish why the area matters
2. **Narrow fast** — compress to the specific unresolved problem
3. **Establish one clearly felt gap** — not a literature review
4. **State the question** — specific enough to kill
5. **Preview the approach and finding** — 1-2 sentences

### Introduction Rules

- Do not turn the introduction into a flat literature review.
- Do not stay broad for more than 2 paragraphs.
- The gap must be felt, not merely listed.
- Each paragraph has one function: stakes, narrowing, gap, question, preview.

## Results

### Structure

Each subsection must answer a **real sub-question**. The order
strengthens the main claim, not mirrors workflow chronology.

### Ordering Principle

1. **Lead with the pattern** — show the phenomenon
2. **Test the mechanism** — explain why
3. **Demonstrate robustness** — stabilize the claim
4. **Mark boundaries** — qualification

This is persuasive order, not chronological order. Never present results
in the order they were produced.

### Results Rules

- Use robustness and qualification to stabilize claims, not to pad.
- Each figure must carry argumentative weight (see
  `figure_role_taxonomy.md`).
- Sub-section titles should name the sub-question, not the method.

## Discussion

### Structure

1. **State what was learned** — synthesize, do not repeat
2. **Enlarge its meaning** — connect to broader questions
3. **Mark the boundaries** — limitations, assumptions, next steps

### Discussion Rules

- Be ambitious in implication and conservative in inference.
- Do not introduce claims that should have been earned in results.
- Do not overstate generality beyond what the evidence covers.
- Qualifications are not weaknesses — they are intellectual honesty.

## Section-Level Flow

Healthy transitions between sections perform one of:

| Move | Function |
|------|----------|
| **narrowing** | from broad to specific |
| **building** | adding evidence to a claim |
| **substantiating** | supporting with robustness |
| **qualifying** | marking limitations |
| **expanding** | extending implications |

Transitions must move the argument. Decorative transitions weaken the
manuscript.
